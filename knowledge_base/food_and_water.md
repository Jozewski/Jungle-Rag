# Food and Water in the Jungle

## The Survival Priority Rule

The human body can survive:
- 3 minutes without air
- 3 hours in extreme cold without shelter
- 3 days without water
- 3 weeks without food

Water is far more urgent than food. Focus on hydration first.

## Finding Water

The jungle is rich with water sources, but not all are safe.

### Running Water
Streams and rivers are your best bet. Always prefer moving water over stagnant pools — moving water has less time to accumulate bacteria and parasites. Even so, always purify before drinking.

### Collecting Rainwater
- Use large leaves as funnels to collect rain directly into a container.
- Stretch a tarp or poncho at an angle and let rain channel to a collection point.
- Bamboo internodes collect rainwater naturally — cut just below a node and you may find fresh water inside.

### Morning Dew
Tie absorbent fabric around your lower legs and walk through vegetation at dawn. Wring the collected dew into your mouth or a container. This can yield 500ml–1L on a good morning.

### Plant Sources
- **Banana plants**: Cut the stem close to the ground, hollow out the stump, and water will seep in. The first filling may be bitter — discard it. Subsequent fills are drinkable.
- **Vines**: Thick vines can carry water. Cut high, then low — hold the lower end to your mouth. Never drink from vines that produce white or colored sap (toxic).
- **Bromeliads**: The cup formed by overlapping leaves collects rainwater. This water may contain insect larvae — strain through cloth.
- **Green bamboo**: Bend a live bamboo stalk, tie it overnight, and cut the tip — water drips from the cut by morning.

## Water Purification

Never drink untreated jungle water, no matter how clear it looks.

### Boiling (Best Method)
Boil water for at least 1 minute (3 minutes at altitude). This kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa.

### Chemical Treatment
- Water purification tablets (iodine or chlorine): Follow package instructions. Wait 30 minutes before drinking.
- Iodine tablets are less effective against Cryptosporidium.

### Improvised Filter
Layer grass, fine sand, charcoal (from your fire), and more sand in a container with holes in the bottom. Pour water through. This removes sediment and improves taste but does NOT kill pathogens — always boil after filtering.

### Solar Disinfection (SODIS)
Fill a clear plastic bottle and leave in direct sunlight for 6+ hours. Less reliable than boiling but better than nothing in an emergency.

## Food in the Jungle

### The Universal Edibility Test
When in doubt, test an unfamiliar plant:
1. Smell it — strong acrid smell = warning sign.
2. Touch juice to the inside of your wrist. Wait 15 minutes. If burning or rash: discard.
3. Touch to your lips. Wait 3 minutes.
4. Touch to your tongue. Wait 3 minutes.
5. Chew a small piece. Spit. Wait 15 minutes.
6. Eat a small amount. Wait 8 hours.

If no reaction at any step, the plant is likely safe in small quantities.

### Safe-to-Eat Jungle Plants
- **Heart of Palm**: The white inner core of young palm trees is safe and nutritious. Taste is mild and starchy.
- **Bamboo shoots**: Young bamboo shoots (under 12 inches) are edible when boiled. Raw shoots contain cyanide compounds — boiling neutralizes them.
- **Wild bananas**: Green bananas are starchy but edible. Ripe bananas are a caloric windfall if you find them.
- **Figs (Ficus species)**: Wild figs are abundant in tropical jungles. Small, purple or yellow when ripe. Highly nutritious.
- **Coconuts**: Immature green coconuts contain electrolyte-rich coconut water and soft, edible flesh.

### Protein Sources
- **Insects**: The most calorie-dense survival food. Termites, grasshoppers, and beetle larvae are all edible. Remove wings and legs from grasshoppers. Cook before eating.
- **Frogs**: Most frogs are edible if cooked. Avoid brightly colored frogs (poison dart frogs) — they are toxic.
- **Fish**: Rivers and streams often contain fish. Construct a weir or improvised spear from sharpened bamboo.
- **Snakes**: Most snakes are edible when cooked. Skin and gut before cooking. Handle dead snakes carefully — bite reflex can persist for hours.

### Plants to AVOID
- Mushrooms: Unless you are a trained mycologist, avoid all jungle mushrooms.
- White-sapped plants: The latex in many jungle plants is toxic (euphorbia species).
- Brightly colored berries: In general, treat as toxic unless positively identified.
- Plants with fine hairs or spines on leaves.

## Fire for Cooking

Never eat raw meat in the jungle. Parasites and bacteria in raw jungle meat can cause debilitating illness. Cook everything thoroughly over fire.

## Caloric Reality

A healthy adult can function on 1,000–1,500 calories per day in survival mode (reduced activity). Prioritize fat and carbohydrate sources for energy. Protein sources are useful but require more energy to digest.
