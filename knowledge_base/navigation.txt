JUNGLE NAVIGATION GUIDE
========================

OVERVIEW
--------
Navigating a jungle is one of the hardest survival challenges. Dense canopy blocks sunlight,
terrain is irregular, and landmarks look the same in every direction. Without proper technique,
even experienced hikers become completely disoriented within minutes. The goal: move deliberately,
leave markers, and trust systems over instinct.


THE CARDINAL RULE: STOP AND THINK FIRST
----------------------------------------
When you realize you are lost, STOP. Sit down. Resist the urge to keep moving — random movement
almost always makes the situation worse. Take a breath, assess what you know:
- When did you last know exactly where you were?
- What direction were you moving?
- What landmarks (rivers, ridgelines, clearings) did you pass?
- How much daylight do you have left?

Only move when you have a plan.


USING THE SUN FOR DIRECTION
-----------------------------
Even in a jungle canopy, brief gaps in the trees reveal the sun's position.

- In the Northern Hemisphere: the sun rises roughly east, sets roughly west, and is due south at noon.
- In the Southern Hemisphere: the sun is due north at noon.
- Hold a stick vertically in sunlight. Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone (this is West).
  Wait 15–20 minutes. Mark the new tip. The second mark is East. A line between them is your East-West axis.
  North is 90 degrees from the East-West line (toward the first mark from your standing position
  facing East).


USING STARS FOR DIRECTION AT NIGHT
------------------------------------
If the canopy allows:
- Northern Hemisphere: Find the North Star (Polaris). It sits directly above magnetic north.
  Locate the Big Dipper — the two stars forming the outer edge of its cup point directly to Polaris.
- Southern Hemisphere: Use the Southern Cross. Extend the long axis of the cross 4.5 times
  in the direction it points — that spot in the sky is due south.


FOLLOWING WATERWAYS
--------------------
Water is your best navigation aid in the jungle. Rivers and streams:
1. Always lead somewhere — to larger rivers, the coast, or human settlements.
2. Provide water, food, and open terrain compared to dense jungle.
3. Are more likely to be traveled by other people.

Follow a stream downhill consistently. Most jungle rivers eventually reach populated coastal areas
or meet larger navigable rivers. Do not cross a river unless you have assessed the current.
Fast-moving water above knee height is dangerous.

IMPORTANT: In mountainous jungles (Amazon headwaters, Borneo), rivers may pass through impassable
gorges. If blocked, backtrack and find a way around the high ground.


TERRAIN FEATURES TO RECOGNIZE
-------------------------------
- Ridgelines: Higher ground means more visibility and sometimes landmarks. Following a ridgeline
  is often easier than fighting through valley brush.
- Saddles: Low points between two peaks — often natural crossing points and animal trails pass through.
- Valleys: Funnel toward water but often densely vegetated and marshy.
- Clearings: Natural clearings may indicate old settlements, landslides, or riverbanks — worth
  investigating for resources and visibility.


MARKING YOUR PATH
------------------
Always mark your trail so you can backtrack:
- Bend or break branches at eye level every 20–30 feet (break toward the direction you came from).
- Stack rocks in cairns at junctions.
- Cut arrow marks into tree bark.
- Tie bright cloth to branches if available.

These markers help rescuers follow your path and allow you to retrace if needed.


MOVING THROUGH DENSE JUNGLE
-----------------------------
- Use a machete or stick to clear vegetation ahead — never reach into unseen brush with your bare hands.
- Move at a 45-degree angle to obstacles rather than trying to push straight through.
- Follow animal trails when possible — they lead to water and represent the path of least resistance.
- Step on logs, not over them — check what's on the other side first (snakes rest on shaded trail sides).
- Move slowly and deliberately. Rushing leads to injuries, which become life-threatening in the jungle.


SETTING BEARINGS
-----------------
If you have a compass:
1. Identify your destination direction.
2. Pick a landmark in that direction (a distinctive tree, rock, or clearing) that you can see.
3. Walk to that landmark.
4. Re-establish your bearing and pick the next landmark.
This "landmark-to-landmark" method keeps you on course through dense vegetation that blocks long sightlines.

Without a compass, use the sun/shadow method to re-establish direction every 30–45 minutes.


RESCUE VISIBILITY
------------------
If you have decided to stay put and wait for rescue:
- Find or create a clearing — a riverbank or hilltop with open sky.
- Create a ground-to-air signal: large letters (SOS, X, HELP) using rocks, logs, or bright material —
  at least 10 feet tall.
- Signal fires: Green vegetation on a hot fire creates thick white smoke visible for miles.
  Three fires in a triangle is the universal distress signal.


DISTANCE ESTIMATION
--------------------
Pace counting: Count every time your left foot hits the ground. For most adults, 100 double-paces =
approximately 150–160 meters. Calibrate your count by pacing a known distance before relying on it.

Time-distance: Through dense jungle, expect to cover only 0.5–1 km per hour. In open terrain,
2–3 km per hour is achievable. Plan accordingly — don't overestimate how far you've traveled.
