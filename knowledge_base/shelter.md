# Jungle Shelter: Building Your Base Camp

## Why Shelter Comes First

In a jungle survival situation, exposure kills faster than hunger or thirst. The jungle environment presents triple threats: rain, humidity, and temperature drop at night. A good shelter can mean the difference between a rested, functional survivor and a hypothermic, insect-ravaged one. Prioritize shelter within the first hour of realizing you are stranded.

## Site Selection

Choose your shelter location carefully. The ideal site is:
- On elevated ground to avoid flash flooding and ground-level insects
- Near water (but not so close that rising water is a risk — at least 200 feet from riverbanks)
- Under a natural canopy that provides overhead cover
- Away from dead trees ("widow makers") that can fall on you
- Not at the bottom of a valley where cold air pools at night

Avoid areas with anthills, wasp nests, or animal trails. Look for signs of wildlife activity before committing to a spot.

## The Lean-To: Fastest Emergency Shelter

The lean-to is the quickest jungle shelter to construct and requires no special materials.

**Steps:**
1. Find two trees 7–10 feet apart and lash a horizontal ridgepole between them at shoulder height using vines or paracord.
2. Cut 10–15 long branches (6–8 feet) and lean them against the ridgepole at a 45-degree angle.
3. Layer large leaves (banana, palm, or elephant ear) from bottom to top on the angled branches, shingle-style. Each layer should overlap the one below by at least 6 inches.
4. Build 4–6 layers of leaves for waterproofing. A properly shingled leaf roof sheds rain effectively.
5. Raise your sleeping platform 6–12 inches off the ground using branches to avoid ground moisture and insects.

A lean-to takes 1–2 hours to build and can protect one or two people.

## The A-Frame: Better Weather Protection

For extended stays or heavy rain:
1. Create a ridgepole running along the ground at the center.
2. Prop it up at one end with a forked stick (3 feet high), leaving the other end on the ground.
3. Lean branches from both sides to create an A shape.
4. Layer leaves as with the lean-to.
5. The enclosed shape retains body heat better and keeps out wind-driven rain.

## Palm-Frond Thatching Technique

Palm fronds are the gold standard for jungle roofing. To thatch effectively:
- Fold fronds over a branch so both halves hang down.
- Start at the bottom of the roof and work upward.
- Each row overlaps the last by 6 inches.
- A double-layered thatch can handle even tropical downpours.
- Weave additional fronds horizontally to lock vertical fronds in place.

## Moisture and Insulation

The jungle floor is wet. Always:
- Sleep on a raised platform or thick leaf litter (6+ inches) to insulate from ground cold.
- Use dry leaves, dry grass, or palm fibers as bedding material.
- Never sleep directly on bare ground — you lose body heat rapidly and invite insects.

## Natural Mosquito Deterrents

- Sleep near a small fire — smoke deters insects.
- Rub citronella leaves (lemongrass) on exposed skin.
- Hang a head net if available.
- Seal gaps in your shelter to reduce mosquito entry.

## Key Rules

- Build before dark — working in jungle darkness is difficult and dangerous.
- Waterproofing is paramount; a wet survivor loses body heat fast.
- Elevation matters — get off the floor.
- Inspect your shelter site for insects and reptiles before settling in.
