JUNGLE SIGNALING AND RESCUE GUIDE
===================================

THE GOLDEN RULE: STAY PUT
--------------------------
Unless you are in immediate danger from flooding, wildlife, or fire, staying in place dramatically
improves your chances of rescue. Rescuers search from your last known location outward. Moving
randomly makes you exponentially harder to find. The exception: if you are near water or a
ridgeline with visibility, move to that position and STAY THERE.

THE RULE OF THREES FOR RESCUE
-------------------------------
The international distress signal uses the number THREE:
- Three fires in a triangle (ideally 30+ feet apart)
- Three blasts on a whistle
- Three flashes of a mirror or light
- Three gunshots (if available)

Repeat signals at regular intervals. Rescuers are trained to listen for repeated signals.


SIGNAL FIRES
-------------
Fire and smoke are your most powerful visual signals.

DAYTIME: Use smoke. Green, leafy material on a hot fire creates thick white smoke. Black smoke
from burning rubber, plastic, or oil is even more visible but use these only if you have them.
Choose a location with open sky above — a riverbank, clearing, or hilltop.

NIGHTTIME: Use flame. A large, bright fire is visible for many miles from aircraft at night.
Keep fuel stocked so you can ignite it immediately upon hearing aircraft.

Three-fire triangle: Space three fires 30+ feet apart in an open area. The triangular pattern
is recognized internationally as a distress signal from the air.

IMPORTANT: Keep a "fast fire" kit — pre-built tinder and kindling bundles so you can ignite
a signal fire within 60 seconds of hearing an aircraft.


GROUND-TO-AIR SIGNALS
-----------------------
If you have time and materials, create large symbols visible from aircraft. Use rocks, logs,
bright clothing, or cleared vegetation contrast. Each symbol should be at LEAST 10 feet tall,
ideally 30+ feet for air visibility.

Standard ground-to-air signals:
  X   = Need medical assistance
  V   = Need help / I need assistance
  ->  = Traveling in this direction
  SOS = Universal distress signal
  N   = No / Negative
  Y   = Yes / Affirmative

Use contrast: light materials on dark ground, or dark materials on light ground.
Visible contrast is more important than the specific material used.


MIRROR SIGNALING
-----------------
A signal mirror can be seen up to 10 miles away on a clear day. If you have any reflective
surface (mirror, phone screen, metallic wrapper, glass):
1. Hold the reflective surface near your face.
2. Extend your other arm and make a peace sign (V) with your fingers.
3. Position the target (aircraft, ship) between your two fingers.
4. Angle the mirror so reflected sunlight passes through your finger V toward the target.
5. Flash repeatedly by tilting the mirror slightly back and forth.

For improvised mirrors: punch a small hole in a foil wrapper. Look through the hole at the
target. Sunlight reflects off the foil around the hole — angle it toward the target.


WHISTLE SIGNALS
----------------
A loud whistle carries far further than the human voice and requires far less energy.
Three blasts, pause, three blasts = distress signal.
One blast = "I am here" / location signal.
Two blasts = "Come to me."

A plastic whistle is one of the highest-value survival items per gram. A Fox 40 or similar
pealess whistle works even when wet.


RIVER AND WATER SIGNALS
------------------------
Rescuers often search along waterways. If you are near a river:
- Create large symbols on sandbars visible from above.
- Hang bright material in trees along the bank.
- Keep a fire burning near the bank.
- Stamp patterns in sand or mud visible from low-flying aircraft.


COMMUNICATION WITH RESCUERS
-----------------------------
If you have a working phone:
- Send your GPS coordinates first (most smartphones show coordinates in the compass or maps app).
- Conserve battery: enable airplane mode when not actively trying to communicate.
- SMS often works when voice calls fail in weak signal areas.
- Emergency services (911, 112 internationally) may be reachable even with zero bars on your carrier.

If you have a satellite messenger (SPOT, Garmin inReach):
- Activate the SOS function and leave it on.
- The device transmits your location every few minutes automatically.
- Move to open sky for best signal.


WHAT RESCUERS LOOK FOR
-----------------------
Search and rescue teams look for:
- Signs of disturbance (broken branches, footprints, cleared areas)
- Smoke
- Bright colors out of place in the jungle
- Movement and reflection
- Three-pattern signals

Help them find you: make your presence large, bright, and distinctive against the jungle background.
The jungle is dark green — your best signals are bright (orange, yellow, silver) or white.


SELF-RESCUE DECISION CRITERIA
-------------------------------
Consider self-rescue (moving toward civilization) only when:
1. You know your location and direction to safety with reasonable certainty.
2. You have water for the expected journey.
3. You are physically capable of the journey.
4. Your shelter location is definitively unsearchable (deep cliff base, underground cave system).
5. A significant amount of time has passed (3+ days) with no sign of search activity.

If any of these conditions are NOT met, stay put and signal.
