# Ocean Survival: Lost at Sea and Shipwreck

## The Opening Moments: Abandoning Ship

A shipwreck unfolds fast. You may have only seconds to minutes before a vessel sinks. Priorities in order:

1. **Send a distress signal** — activate the EPIRB (Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon) immediately. If no EPIRB, send a Mayday on VHF Channel 16 with your position before anything else.
2. **Get your survival kit** — ditch bag with water, flares, mirror, knife, fishing line.
3. **Inflate and board the life raft** — always step UP into a life raft, never jump into the water next to it.
4. **Cut the painter line** — once everyone is aboard, cut the line connecting the raft to the sinking vessel. A sinking ship creates suction that can pull the raft down with it.
5. **Move away from the vessel** — 100+ meters to avoid suction, debris, and fuel slicks.

**Never go into the water if you can avoid it.** Cold water kills in minutes. Even warm water (70°F / 21°C) causes significant heat loss over hours. A life raft, no matter how uncomfortable, is infinitely preferable to open water.

## The Brutal Rule: Never Drink Seawater

Seawater contains 3.5% salt — three times the concentration your kidneys can process. Drinking it accelerates dehydration by forcing your kidneys to extract water from your cells to excrete the excess salt. It causes rapid kidney failure, hallucinations, and death. This is absolute: **never drink seawater, urine, or fish blood.**

## Water at Sea

Dehydration is the primary killer after shipwreck. A person lost at sea can survive roughly:
- 3 days without water in hot conditions
- 7–10 days in cooler conditions

### Collecting Rainwater

Rainfall is your primary water source. Set up collection systems:
- Spread any available material (tarp, clothing, sail) to funnel rain.
- Many life rafts have built-in rain catchers in the canopy.
- Rinse collection surfaces first — salt spray contaminates collection materials.
- Drink rain as it falls — don't wait to collect it.
- Store in every available container.

### Dew Collection

In calm conditions with clear nights, dew forms on the raft canopy. Wipe surfaces at dawn with cloth and wring into a container.

### Solar Still at Sea

A floating solar still (if available in your kit) uses sunlight to evaporate seawater and condense fresh water. Commercial units produce 1–2 liters per day. Improvised still: fill a plastic bag with a small amount of seawater, seal it, leave in sun — condensation of evaporated water (not the seawater directly) is collected.

### Water Rationing

Ration strictly from day one:
- 500ml (about 2 cups) per day keeps you alive
- 1 liter per day with reduced sun exposure is more comfortable
- No water ration for first 24 hours if water is truly unknown — the body has reserves

Reduce physical activity to minimize sweating. Rest during peak sun hours under the raft canopy.

## Food at Sea

You can survive weeks without food. Do not eat if you do not have adequate water — digestion requires fluids and will accelerate dehydration. With water secured:

### Fish

Fish are the life-raft survivor's primary food source. Fish congregate in open ocean beneath floating objects (like your raft) for shade and protection — within 24–72 hours, you'll have company.

**Fishing techniques**:
- Improvise hooks from safety pins, wire, bent nails, or even reflective material bent into a J shape.
- Use anything shiny as a lure — foil wrappers, buttons, reflective tape.
- Fish are attracted to light at night — dangling a flashlight at the surface draws them.
- Dorado (mahi-mahi), flying fish, and various tuna species commonly approach life rafts.
- Flying fish sometimes land on the raft at night — collect them.

**Preparing fish without fire**:
- Eat fish raw if necessary — it contains significant moisture as well as protein.
- Remove the gut immediately to prevent spoilage.
- Do not eat fish liver in large quantities (toxic levels of vitamin A in some species).
- Fresh fish eyes contain drinkable moisture — an important hydration source.
- Fish blood is salty — do not drink it as a substitute for water.

### Seabirds

Seabirds that land on the raft can be caught by hand (approach slowly, grab quickly). All seabirds are edible when cooked, and edible raw in extremis. Their blood is salty — do not drink it. Their flesh contains moisture.

### Sea Turtles

Sea turtles can be caught if they approach the raft. All sea turtle species are now protected, but survival takes legal precedence. Turtle blood is less salty than seawater and has been used as emergency hydration. Turtle meat is edible raw or cooked.

### Seaweed

Floating seaweed (Sargassum in the Atlantic) is edible raw and contains some nutrients. It also attracts small fish and crabs. However, it has minimal caloric and hydration value.

## Exposure and Sun

The sun on open water is devastating. Without shade:
- Sunburn causes fluid loss and severely degrades skin integrity, inviting infection.
- UV reflection off water doubles sun exposure.
- Heat exhaustion is rapid.

Always stay under the life raft canopy during daylight hours. Wear all available clothing — coverage reduces evaporative water loss and UV exposure. Keep a wet cloth on exposed skin to reduce temperature.

**Salt sores**: Salt spray causes open sores on skin exposed to constant moisture. Keep skin as dry as possible inside the raft. Change positions regularly to prevent prolonged contact with salt water that pools in the raft bottom.

## Seasickness

Seasickness is debilitating in open-water survival. Vomiting causes dangerous fluid and electrolyte loss.

- Fixes gaze on the horizon.
- Lay down flat in the raft — this reduces the visual motion stimulus.
- Avoid reading or looking at detailed objects.
- Antihistamines (meclizine/Dramamine) in the survival kit are extremely valuable.
- Ginger (if available) reduces nausea.
- Most people adapt to raft motion within 2–3 days.

## Navigation at Sea

### Estimating Direction

**Stars**: The North Star (Polaris) is due north — the most reliable nighttime navigation reference in the Northern Hemisphere. The Southern Cross points south in the Southern Hemisphere.

**Sun**: Rises east, sets west. Solar noon (highest point) is due south (Northern Hemisphere) or due north (Southern Hemisphere).

**Swell direction**: Ocean swells generally run in consistent directions determined by dominant winds. Once you identify swell direction, it can be used as a compass reference throughout the day.

**Wind**: Trade winds blow east-to-west in tropical latitudes. Prevailing westerlies blow west-to-east in temperate latitudes. Knowing the general trade wind pattern helps orient direction.

### Estimating Distance to Land

**Birds**: Increasing numbers of seabirds (especially land-based species like frigatebirds, boobies) suggest proximity to land. Frigate birds cannot land on water and roost on land — their presence suggests land within 100 miles.

**Clouds**: Stationary cumulus clouds over a flat horizon often indicate land underneath — islands create updrafts that generate clouds.

**Color of water**: The open ocean is deep blue. Greenish or lighter water indicates shallow bottom — possible reef or coastal shelf ahead.

**Debris**: Fresh plant material, soil-stained logs, or river foam in the water indicates a river mouth nearby — and therefore land.

**Phosphorescence**: Doesn't indicate land but can help: at night, breaking waves create phosphorescent glow. If waves break on an unseen reef or shoal, phosphorescence marks the hazard.

## Signaling at Sea

You are a tiny object in an enormous ocean. Signaling is critical and continuous.

### EPIRB (Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon)

The most important item on a vessel. When activated, transmits your position to satellite and rescuers within hours. Most rescues at sea happen within 24 hours of EPIRB activation. If you have one, activate it immediately.

### Pyrotechnic Flares

- **Parachute flares**: Shoot 300m high, burn for 40 seconds. Use when you can hear or see an aircraft or vessel.
- **Hand flares**: Burn at eye level for 60 seconds. Useful for signaling position to vessels at night.
- **Smoke signals**: Orange smoke — for daytime, highly visible on the water.

Conserve flares — use only when a vessel or aircraft is confirmed nearby. Three flares in sequence is the international distress signal.

### Signal Mirror

A heliograph mirror is visible from aircraft up to 10 miles away. Aim using the V-sighting technique (see navigation guide). Even a spoon or reflective wrapper works. Practice the technique before you need it.

### Dye Marker

Fluorescent green or orange sea dye turns a large area of ocean a vivid color — highly visible from aircraft. Deploy when you hear aircraft overhead.

### VHF Radio

Channel 16 is monitored by all vessels and coast guard worldwide. Mayday call: "Mayday, Mayday, Mayday — this is [vessel name], position [coordinates], [nature of distress], [number of people], [description of vessel]. I require immediate assistance." Repeat every 30 minutes.

## Physical and Psychological Endurance

### Immersion Foot (Salt Water)

Prolonged exposure to salt water causes immersion foot (similar to trench foot). Feet swell, crack, and become intensely painful. Rinse feet with fresh water (precious as it is), dry them, and elevate them. The raft floor's constant moisture is unavoidable — change positions frequently.

### Psychological Challenges

Survival at sea involves profound isolation, helplessness, and monotony punctuated by terror. Famous survivors describe days of numbness interrupted by violent storms or close rescues that didn't happen.

**Maintain routine**: Set watch schedules (2-hour shifts if multiple survivors). Assign tasks. Look for food and water at regular intervals. Document each day. The structure preserves sanity.

**Hope is not naive**: Most people who are reported missing at sea are found. Search and rescue operations expand outward systematically. If you have activated an EPIRB, rescue is likely within 24–72 hours. If you have not, your odds decrease significantly over time — but documented survivors have lasted 75+ days at sea on a life raft.

## Famous Survival Cases (Inspiring Facts)

- **Steven Callahan** survived 76 days alone on a life raft in 1982 after his boat sank, crossing from the Canary Islands toward the Caribbean. He lost 1/3 of his body weight but survived using fishing, solar still, and meticulous rationing.
- **Poon Lim** survived 133 days alone on a wooden raft in 1943 after his merchant ship was torpedoed in the Atlantic — the longest solo open-ocean survival on record.
- **The Baileys** survived 117 days in 1973 after their sailboat sank, living on fish, turtles, and rainwater.

These cases demonstrate that human survival at sea is possible far beyond what intuition suggests, given the right knowledge and determination.

## Sea Survival Kit Essentials (If You Can Grab One Item Per Second)

1. EPIRB or PLB (personal locator beacon)
2. Water (sealed containers)
3. Flares
4. Signal mirror
5. VHF radio
6. Knife
7. Fishing kit (hooks, line, lures)
8. Sunscreen and hat
9. Water purification tablets
10. First aid kit with seasickness medication
