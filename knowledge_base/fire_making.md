# Making Fire in the Jungle

## Why Fire is Critical

Fire serves five survival functions in the jungle:
1. **Purifies water** — boiling is the most reliable purification method
2. **Cooks food** — raw jungle meat contains dangerous parasites
3. **Warmth** — nights in the jungle can drop to 60–65°F, causing hypothermia in wet clothes
4. **Signals** — smoke is visible for miles; three fires in a triangle is the international distress signal
5. **Morale** — fire provides psychological comfort in a survival situation

## The Core Challenge: Moisture

Jungle humidity is the enemy of fire. Everything is damp. Your primary goal before striking a spark is finding or creating dry fuel.

## Finding Dry Tinder

Even in the wettest jungle, dry material exists:
- **Inside dead bamboo**: Split dead bamboo stalks — the inner fibers are often dry.
- **Under bark**: The inner bark of dead trees stays dry. Shred it into fine fibers.
- **Abandoned bird nests**: Made of fine fibers that dry quickly.
- **Your clothing**: Lint from pockets or seams. Even slightly damp fabric can work if ignition is strong.
- **Inner heartwood of dead logs**: The center of a rotting log is often dry.
- **Dry leaves**: Look under overhanging rocks, large leaves, or forest canopy — anywhere rain doesn't reach.

The finer the tinder, the better. Your tinder bundle should be the texture of a bird's nest — fine, fluffy material in the center, coarser fibers around it.

## The Fire Triangle

You need three elements:
1. **Heat (ignition source)**
2. **Fuel (dry wood)**
3. **Oxygen (don't smother it)**

## Ignition Methods

### 1. Friction Fire: Bow Drill (Most Reliable Without Tools)

**Materials needed:**
- **Fireboard**: Flat piece of dry softwood (cedar, willow, cottonwood, or local equivalents). Roughly 12 inches long, 1 inch thick.
- **Spindle**: Straight, dry hardwood or medium-hardwood stick, 12–18 inches long, 3/4 inch diameter. Straight is critical.
- **Bow**: Curved branch with paracord, shoelace, or vine strung loosely between ends.
- **Handhold**: Hard, smooth material (hardwood, stone, shell) to press on spindle top.
- **Notch and depression**: Carve a depression in the fireboard, then cut a V-shaped notch from the depression to the edge so coal falls out.

**Technique:**
1. Place a leaf or bark fragment under the notch to catch the coal.
2. Kneel with one foot on the fireboard.
3. Wrap the bow string once around the spindle.
4. Press the spindle into the depression with the handhold.
5. Apply steady downward pressure.
6. Bow back and forth at moderate speed — not too fast.
7. Black powder (coal) collects in the notch. When smoke thickens, you're close.
8. When a coal forms, tap it into your tinder bundle.
9. Cradle the tinder bundle, blow gently and steadily into the coal.
10. The bundle ignites — add to your prepared fire structure.

This method requires practice. Do not give up after one attempt.

### 2. Hand Drill (Simpler, Harder)
Same principle as bow drill but use your hands to spin the spindle. Requires very dry materials and significant technique. Works best in dry climates — harder in humid jungle conditions.

### 3. Flint and Steel (With Proper Stone)
If you find flint, chert, quartz, or obsidian:
- Strike at a steep angle against the back of a steel blade or belt buckle.
- Direct sparks into a char cloth or very fine, dry tinder.
- Once a spark lands, fold tinder over it and blow steadily.

### 4. Magnifying Lens (Sunlight Required)
Use eyeglasses, a camera lens, a clear plastic bag of water, or a magnifying glass to focus sunlight onto dark-colored dry tinder. Works only in direct sunlight — which the jungle canopy often blocks.

### 5. Lighter or Matches
Always protect these. Keep in a waterproof container. If wet, dry matches under your armpit for 30 minutes before attempting to strike.

## Building Your Fire Structure

Never light a match until you have all three layers ready:

**Layer 1 — Tinder**: Fine, dry material (size of pencil width and smaller). Enough to fill two cupped hands.

**Layer 2 — Kindling**: Small twigs and sticks, thumb-thickness or less. 20–30 pieces.

**Layer 3 — Fuel**: Larger sticks and wood. Progressively larger pieces.

**The Teepee Structure**:
- Make a small nest of tinder.
- Build kindling in a teepee above it, leaving a "door" gap to access the tinder.
- Ignite tinder, close the gap gently.
- As kindling catches, add fuel pieces to the outside.

**Log Cabin Structure** (burns longer, better for sustained cooking fires):
- Build two parallel logs.
- Lay two more perpendicular across them.
- Continue stacking like a log cabin, with tinder and kindling in the center.

## Fire Safety in the Jungle

- Always clear debris in a 3-foot radius of your fire.
- Never leave a fire unattended.
- Prepare water to douse the fire before sleeping.
- Build a reflector wall (row of logs or stones on one side) to direct heat toward your shelter.
- Jungle fires can spread through dried leaf litter — monitor constantly.

## Keeping Fire Going

- Hardwood burns longer and produces more coals.
- Never burn green wood as your main fuel — it produces excessive smoke and little heat.
- Maintain a coal bed — a fire kept at coals is easier to re-ignite than starting from scratch.
- Cover coals with ash overnight to preserve them. In the morning, blow on them and add kindling.
