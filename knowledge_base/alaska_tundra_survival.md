# Alaska and Tundra Survival Guide

## The Cold Environment Threat Model

Alaska and the arctic tundra present an inverse challenge to the desert: here the environment is trying to steal your heat, not overwhelm you with it. Cold, wet, and wind are the triple threat. Hypothermia — the dangerous drop in core body temperature — is the primary cause of death in cold environments. It can kill within hours even at temperatures well above freezing when combined with wind and moisture.

The arctic tundra spans Alaska, northern Canada, Siberia, Scandinavia, and Greenland. It is characterized by permafrost (permanently frozen ground), sparse vegetation, bogs and tundra ponds in summer, and brutal wind exposure year-round. There are no trees on the open tundra — this dramatically affects shelter and fire options.

## Understanding Hypothermia

Core body temperature must stay above 95°F (35°C) to function. Hypothermia begins below this threshold.

**Mild hypothermia (89–95°F / 32–35°C)**: Shivering (the body's heat-generating mechanism), slurred speech, stumbling, impaired judgment. The person may feel paradoxically warm and attempt to remove clothing — this is a dangerous sign.

**Moderate hypothermia (82–89°F / 28–32°C)**: Shivering stops (the body is exhausted). Muscle stiffness, profound confusion, severe drowsiness. The person may appear drunk. This is life-threatening.

**Severe hypothermia (below 82°F / 28°C)**: Loss of consciousness, very slow or absent pulse. May appear dead. The famous rule: "A person is not dead until they are warm and dead" — rewarming has revived people with no detectable pulse. Treat all hypothermia victims as salvageable.

### Rewarming Protocol
1. Move out of cold/wind/wet immediately.
2. Remove wet clothing and replace with dry insulation.
3. Insulate from ground — conductive heat loss to frozen ground is massive.
4. Apply heat to core: warm packs, body heat from another person, or warm (not hot) beverages if conscious.
5. Do NOT rub limbs — this drives cold blood to the core.
6. Do NOT give alcohol — it dilates blood vessels and accelerates heat loss.
7. Handle gently — the cold heart is susceptible to fibrillation from jarring movement.

## Wind Chill

Wind chill is the felt temperature accounting for heat lost to wind. At 0°F (-18°C) with a 30 mph wind, the felt temperature is -26°F (-32°C) — frostbite can occur in 10 minutes. Wind chill tables must be understood to assess real danger:

- 0°F calm = dangerous
- 0°F + 20 mph wind = extremely dangerous (exposed skin freezes in 30 min)
- -20°F + 20 mph wind = life-threatening (exposed skin freezes in 10 min)

Any structure that blocks wind dramatically reduces heat loss. Even a shallow depression in snow cuts wind and saves lives.

## Frostbite

Frostbite is the freezing of tissue. Most common in fingers, toes, ears, nose, and cheeks.

**Frostnip**: Skin becomes pale, waxy, and numb but not frozen through. Rewarm with body heat (hands, armpits). No lasting damage if caught early.

**Superficial frostbite**: Ice crystals form in the skin layer. Skin is white/grey and hard on the surface but soft underneath. After rewarming, intense burning pain and blistering occur. Protect blisters from breaking.

**Deep frostbite**: Tissue frozen solid. Gray or black, no sensation, wooden to the touch. Do NOT rewarm in the field if there is any chance of re-freezing — refreezing causes far greater damage than leaving frozen. Walk out on frozen feet if necessary (once rewarmed, they cannot bear weight due to pain and damage).

**Rewarming frostbite**: Only if no re-freezing risk. Immerse in water heated to exactly 104°F (40°C) — not hotter. This is extremely painful. Elevate and protect the rewarmed tissue.

## Shelter in Alaska and the Tundra

### Priority: Wind Protection First

In the open tundra, wind is more dangerous than cold alone. A shelter that provides complete wind protection while retaining body heat is your first goal.

### The Snow Trench (30 minutes to build)

The fastest cold-weather emergency shelter:
1. Dig a trench in snow just wider than your body and deep enough to be below the surface (3–4 feet).
2. Length: slightly longer than you are tall.
3. Cover the top with a layer of packed snow blocks, branches, or a tarp — creating a roof.
4. Crawl in and seal the entrance with your pack, snow blocks, or clothing.
5. Body heat warms the interior rapidly. Snow is an excellent insulator — inside temperature rises to 32°F (0°C) within 20 minutes, which is dramatically warmer than -20°F wind chill outside.

### The Quinzhee (2–4 hours to build)

Better than a trench for extended stays:
1. Pile snow into a mound 8–10 feet tall and 12 feet wide. Pack it loosely.
2. Insert 10–15 sticks, 8 inches long, around the mound as depth gauges.
3. Wait 2 hours for the snow to sinter (bond) — critical step.
4. Hollow out the interior, leaving 8 inches of wall thickness (when you hit the sticks, stop).
5. Make a ventilation hole in the ceiling with a stick.
6. Excavate a raised sleeping platform inside — cold air pools at the lowest point.

### The Snow Cave (Best, but site-dependent)

Requires a deep snowbank or hillside. Dig horizontally into the bank, then angle upward to create an interior chamber. The upward angle traps warm air. Add ventilation hole, raised sleeping platform. Interior stays at 32°F regardless of outside temperature. This is the gold standard of arctic shelter.

### Forest Shelter (if trees are present)

In Alaska's boreal forest (taiga), dense spruce trees provide:
- Natural windbreaks
- Branches for insulation bedding
- Fuel for fire
- Boughs for a debris hut

A lean-to against a large spruce, combined with a reflector fire, is extremely effective. Spruce boughs stacked 6 inches deep make excellent insulating bedding.

## Fire in Cold Environments

Fire is more critical in cold environments than any other — for warmth, water (melting snow), signaling, and morale.

### The Cold Challenge

Cold saps manual dexterity. Practice fire-making with gloves on — your hands may be too cold for fine motor tasks when you most need fire.

### Fuel Sources

**Boreal forest (Alaska, northern Canada)**: Dead standing spruce and birch are ideal. Dead wood on the ground in tundra conditions is often wet — look for dead branches still on standing trees. Birch bark is excellent tinder — the oils in it ignite even when wet.

**Open tundra**: Willow and alder shrubs along stream banks. Dried tundra vegetation and sedge grasses can serve as tinder. Dry animal dung (if near caribou areas) burns hot.

**Fatwood**: Resin-impregnated heartwood of dead conifers. Looks yellowish or orange, smells like pine pitch. Burns intensely even when wet. Search dead stumps and root bases.

### Fire Platform

In deep snow, always build a fire on a platform of green logs — a standard fire melts its way down through snow and extinguishes. A 4-inch thick platform of green logs holds the fire above the snow surface.

### Wind Protection

Wind kills fire. Always build behind a windbreak — a snow wall, rock, or log. Cup your hands around tinder while lighting. Build the fire structure before striking any match.

## Water in Cold Environments

### Snow and Ice

**Snow**: Snow is dilute — it takes enormous quantities to produce small amounts of water. Melting snow in a pot over fire is effective but requires fuel. You need roughly 10 cups of snow to produce 1 cup of water. Yellow or gray snow may be contaminated. Never eat large quantities of snow — the energy cost of melting it in your body exceeds its hydration value and accelerates hypothermia.

**Ice**: Clear river or lake ice is denser than snow and produces more water per volume. Be extremely cautious around ice — test with a stick before venturing onto frozen water.

**Running water**: Streams and rivers in Alaska run year-round even in winter (under ice). Find open water at rapids, bends, or where springs feed streams.

**Water purification**: Even in the arctic, water from streams may contain Giardia (from beaver and other wildlife). Boil all water for at least 1 minute.

### Recognizing Dangerous Ice

Ice safety by thickness:
- Under 4 inches: Stay off
- 4–6 inches: Single person on foot
- 8–12 inches: Snowmobile
- 12+ inches: Small vehicle

Test ice with a spud bar or axe handle by striking and listening for hollow sounds. Opaque white ice is weaker than clear blue-green ice. Ice near outlets, inlets, and springs is weakest.

**If you fall through ice**:
1. Don't panic. Kick your feet and use your arms to pull forward onto the ice edge.
2. Roll away from the hole — do not stand (weight distribution).
3. Move immediately to shelter and warmth. You have 10–15 minutes of useful movement before incapacitation.

## Food in the Arctic

Caloric needs in cold environments increase dramatically — the body burns 4,000–6,000 calories daily in extreme cold. Fat is the most calorie-dense food and the most valuable.

**Fish**: Alaska's rivers and lakes are extraordinarily fish-rich. Ice fishing: find open water or chip through ice. Simple hooks and line fashioned from wire or safety pins can catch fish. Grayling, char, and trout bite readily.

**Rabbits (snowshoe hare)**: Abundant in boreal forest. Set snares at their trail runs — hares follow predictable paths in snow. Use flexible wire bent into a loop (4 inches diameter) set 4 inches above the trail. Attach to a bent sapling trigger.

**Ptarmigan and grouse**: Ground-dwelling birds that remain in tundra/boreal forest year-round. They are often surprisingly tame in winter. Snares or throwing sticks can be effective.

**Caribou**: Migrating herds cross the tundra. Caribou provide enormous quantities of meat, fat, and hide (for insulation). Hunting them requires patience and stealth.

**Berries**: In late summer/early fall, Alaska's tundra is covered in blueberries, cloudberries, and crowberries. These are rich in vitamin C and antioxidants. Many freeze on the vine — available throughout fall.

**Birch sap**: In early spring, birch trees run with sweet sap — tap by drilling a small hole and inserting a tube. Yields 1–3 liters per day. Contains sugars and some nutrients.

## Navigation in Snow and Tundra

### Whiteout Conditions

A whiteout occurs when overcast sky and snow-covered ground have the same luminosity — no shadows, no horizon, no depth perception. It is profoundly disorienting. In a whiteout: **stop moving**. Attempting to walk in a whiteout risks walking off cliffs, into rivers, or in circles. Build a shelter and wait.

### Landmarks and Terrain Association

Tundra appears featureless but has subtle landmarks: river drainage patterns, ridgelines, eskers (long gravel ridges left by glaciers), and vegetation patterns. Study a map before entering tundra — know your river system.

### GPS and Compass

In Alaska, a GPS unit is the most important navigation tool. Carry extra batteries — cold drains batteries rapidly. Keep batteries in an inner pocket next to your body. A compass functions in all conditions — the magnetic pole wanders significantly in Alaska (up to 20° declination) — account for this.

### Aurora and Stars

The Northern Lights (aurora borealis) run roughly east-west, often indicating direction. The North Star (Polaris) is directly above true north, highly useful at night. Clear arctic nights offer extraordinary star visibility.

## Wildlife in Alaska

**Brown and Black Bears**: Most dangerous in spring (hungry after hibernation) and with cubs. Make noise while traveling. Carry bear spray (more effective than guns at close range). If charged by a brown bear: play dead, face down, hands protecting neck. If attacked by a black bear: fight back aggressively.

**Moose**: Responsible for more injuries in Alaska than bears. Cow moose with calves are extremely aggressive. If a moose charges: run and put a large tree between you and it. Unlike bears, moose will not pursue far.

**Wolves**: Rarely attack humans but can be aggressive in remote areas, especially when habituated to people. Make noise and appear large.

**Hypothermia from water**: Cold water immersion is extremely dangerous. At 35°F (2°C) water temperature, you have roughly 30 minutes of consciousness. Swim only if absolutely necessary. Float on your back, minimize movement, and wait for rescue.

## Key Alaska/Tundra Rules

1. **Cotton kills** — wet cotton has zero insulating value. Wool and synthetic fabrics retain warmth when wet.
2. **Layer system**: Base (moisture-wicking), mid (insulating), outer (windproof/waterproof).
3. **"If you're sweating, you're dying"**: Overheating causes sweating → wet base layer → rapid chilling when activity stops. Vent layers before sweating.
4. **Keep moving to generate heat** — but rest before exhaustion.
5. **Protect your extremities**: Frostbitten hands cannot start fires or build shelter.
6. **Tell someone your plan** before entering remote Alaska — search and rescue is measured in days, not hours.
7. **Carry extra food**: Cold weather burns 2–3x normal calories.
